Smoking and Lung Cancer in Australia

Australia has cut its smoking rate to one of the lowest in the world, yet lung cancer is still the country's deadliest cancer. This is the story of where the risk lives, who carries it, and how it has shifted over the decades.

10.6% of Australian adults smoked daily in 2022, down from over a third in 1980.
9,191 Australians died of lung cancer in 2022, the country's deadliest cancer.
70% fall in the smoking rate since 1980, but lung cancer is only just beginning to follow.

Where does Australia smoke?

Smoking is not evenly spread across the country. Remote and regional areas , particularly in the Northern Territory, carry rates well above the national average, while the cities of New South Wales, Victoria and the ACT sit at the low end. But because population is concentrated along the east coast, more than half of all Australian smokers live in just two states: New South Wales and Victoria.

The cost of smoking

Lung cancer is the fifth most common cancer in Australia, but the first cause of cancer death. And smoking's footprint reaches well beyond the lungs: it drives a third of all lung cancer burden, and substantial shares of heart disease, stroke, COPD and several other cancers.

How has it changed over the decades?

Smoking peaked in Australia in the 1960s and has fallen steadily ever since. Lung cancer incidence followed, but only after a long delay, because the disease takes decades to develop after sustained exposure.

Why lung cancer is so feared

Lung cancer is unusual among common cancers: it kills more than two in three of those diagnosed in the same year. Most Australian cancers are far less lethal, for prostate or breast cancer the share is well under one in five.

Who is most at risk?

Risk is shaped by gender and age. Australian men still smoke more and are diagnosed with lung cancer at higher rates than women, though the gap has narrowed sharply over the last twenty years.

Decades of delay

The lag between smoking and disease is not just visible nationally; it shows up in every age band. Smoking peaks in middle age, but lung cancer rates only rise sharply two or three decades later.

The bottom line

Lower smoking rates have already prevented thousands of lung cancer cases, but because of the long lag, the largest gains for today's smokers are still decades away. To compare two measures on very different scales, each series here is indexed to its own first reading (= 100): the lines show how much each has changed, not their raw units.
